# Content Expansion Ladder

## In a Nutshell

Start every content idea at the smallest viable format. Only expand to a bigger format when you see real demand signals at the current level. Most ideas live and die as tweets — and that's fine. The ladder prevents the biggest content time-waste: writing long-form pieces nobody asked for.

## Source

Original — synthesized from "tiny bets" thinking, lean startup validation, and hard-won experience writing blog posts that got 12 views.

## How It Works

The ladder has six rungs. You start at the bottom and only climb when the audience pulls you up.

| Rung | Format | Effort | Time |
|------|--------|--------|------|
| 0 | Captured note / idea | None (private) | 30 sec |
| 1 | Tweet / short post | Minimal | 2 min |
| 2 | Thread / LinkedIn post | Low | 15-30 min |
| 3 | Blog post | Medium | 2-4 hrs |
| 4 | Blog series / guide | High | Days |
| 5 | Video / course / product | Highest | Weeks |

### Demand signals — how you know it's time to climb

| Current rung | Climb when you see... |
|---|---|
| 0 → 1 | You think "this might be interesting to others" |
| 1 → 2 | Replies, quote tweets, saves, unexpectedly high engagement |
| 2 → 3 | Follow-up questions, DMs saying "tell me more," people sharing with their own commentary |
| 3 → 4 | Search traffic to the post, newsletter signups from it, people linking to it, "where can I read more about X?" |
| 4 → 5 | Sustained inbound interest over months, people referencing it in their own content, offers to pay |

### The "climb or stay" test

At each rung, ask:

- Did anyone engage beyond a passive like?
- Did anyone ask for more?
- Did anyone share it with their own audience?
- Is there evidence of **pull** (people coming to you) vs. **push** (you promoting it)?

If the answer is mostly no, the idea stays where it is. No guilt.

### How this coexists with "site-first" content

This ladder is for **unplanned ideas** — observations, opinions, small insights that come up during the day. You don't know upfront if they're worth a full piece, so you test small.

For **planned craft content** (Weekend Builds, Dead End Builds, Essays), go straight to the site. You already know those will be full pieces. The site is still the hub — and when a ladder idea climbs to rung 3+, it lands on the site too.

Think of it this way: site-first is top-down (start big, distribute small). The ladder is bottom-up (start small, expand if earned). Both are valid — they just apply to different types of content.

### What the ladder is NOT

- **Not a content calendar.** There's no schedule. You don't owe anyone a thread just because a tweet did well.
- **Not a growth hack.** Climbing is a response to genuine pull, not a strategy to engineer virality.
- **Not a guilt machine.** Most ideas never climb past rung 1. That's the system working correctly — it filtered out the ideas that weren't worth more time.

## When to Use

- When you have an idea and aren't sure if it's worth a blog post — tweet it first
- When you're tempted to write a 2,000-word piece on something you've never talked about publicly — test the core idea as a tweet instead
- When deciding what to write next — look at which tweets/posts got unexpected traction
- As a defense against the content treadmill — only invest serious time in ideas that have already proven demand
- When reviewing your captured notes — which ones are worth promoting to rung 1?

## Example

You notice something surprising while building with Claude — it handles a specific pattern way better than expected. Instead of spending 3 hours writing a blog post about it:

1. **Rung 0:** Jot it down in your notes
2. **Rung 1:** Tweet: "TIL Claude handles [pattern] surprisingly well. Was expecting to need [workaround] but it just... worked."
3. The tweet gets 40 replies and people asking "what prompt did you use?" and "does this work for [related thing]?"
4. **Rung 2:** Write a thread walking through the setup, the prompt, and the result
5. The thread gets shared by 3 people with their own commentary. Someone DMs asking if you've written about this.
6. **Rung 3:** Now you write the blog post. You already know the angle (the surprising part), the questions people have (from replies), and that there's real interest.

Compare this to writing the blog post first — where you'd be guessing what resonates. The ladder version took the same total effort but each step was validated.
