# Tiny Bets

## In a Nutshell

Don't bet big on one thing. Place many small, low-risk bets across different ideas, products, and channels. Let the winners reveal themselves. Kill the losers fast. Double down only on what's already working.

## Source

Daniel Vassallo — former Amazon engineer who quit a $500K/year job to build a portfolio of small bets. Books, info products, SaaS, courses — all small, all launched fast, most failures, a few big wins. His thesis: in an uncertain world, diversification beats conviction.

Key idea: you cannot predict what will work. The market decides. Your job is to generate enough shots on goal that the odds are in your favor, then recognize and amplify the winners.

## How It Works

The framework has three phases:

### 1. Generate bets

Ship small things fast. A weekend build. A tweet thread. A landing page. A $10 product. The cost of each bet should be low enough that failure is painless. If it takes more than a few days or a few hundred dollars, it's too big to be a bet — it's a commitment.

### 2. Measure signal

Watch for pull — not push. Did anyone share it? Did anyone ask for more? Did anyone pay? Vanity metrics (likes, views) are noise. Signal is when people take action: sign up, buy, DM you, share with their audience.

### 3. Allocate based on results

- **No signal →** let it sit or kill it. No guilt. Most bets lose. That's the system working.
- **Weak signal →** keep it alive at minimum cost. Revisit later.
- **Strong signal →** invest more. Iterate. Market it. This is now worth real time.

The key discipline: resist the urge to over-invest before you have signal. The builder's instinct is to keep polishing. The tiny bets discipline says: ship it ugly, see if anyone cares, then decide.

## When to Use

- When deciding what to build next — start with the smallest version, not the grand vision
- When a project isn't getting traction — ask whether you're forcing a losing bet
- When you feel pressure to commit months to one thing — that's the signal to diversify instead
- When allocating your limited maker time across properties — spread bets, concentrate winners
- When planning content — test ideas as tweets before committing to long-form
- When evaluating new opportunities — what's the minimum bet I can place to learn?

## Example

My property portfolio is tiny bets in action. Eleven products across iOS apps, macOS utilities, web tools, and content sites. Most are in early stages — low investment, testing signal. The ones that show traction (Ration growing, Please Mr Pig getting App Store-ready) earn more time. The ones that don't (Respite, MonoCal) sit in the parking lot. No guilt. The portfolio *is* the bet — any one of these could be the winner, but I don't have to guess which one upfront.

Same logic applies at the content level: a tweet that gets traction becomes a thread, which becomes a blog post. Start small, let the audience tell you what to expand.
