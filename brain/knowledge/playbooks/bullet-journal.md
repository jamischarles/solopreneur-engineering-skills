# Bullet Journal

## In a Nutshell

An analog organization system designed by someone with ADHD, for anyone who needs to manage scattered attention. The core mechanic is *rapid logging* — a minimalist notation for tasks, events, and notes — combined with *migration*, the act of regularly reviewing and rewriting what still matters. The friction of rewriting is the feature: it forces you to ask "is this still worth my time?"

## Source

Ryder Carroll — *The Bullet Journal Method: Track the Past, Order the Present, Design the Future* (2018). Carroll developed the system to manage his own ADHD. It's intentionally analog — the constraints of pen and paper prevent the digital rabbit holes that derail attention.

## The Framework

### 1. Rapid Logging

Everything goes into the journal as bullets with simple signifiers:
- `*` Task (something to do)
- `o` Event (something that happened or is scheduled)
- `-` Note (a thought, idea, or observation)
- `>` Migrated (moved forward to next month/week)
- `x` Completed
- `~` Struck through (no longer relevant)

Write fast. Don't elaborate. The constraint of brevity forces clarity.

### 2. Collections

Themed pages for specific topics — a project plan, a reading list, a brainstorm. Collections give structure to related items without forcing everything into a single chronological stream.

### 3. Migration

The engine of the system. At the end of each month (or week):
1. Review every open task
2. For each one, decide: **migrate** (carry forward — it still matters), **schedule** (give it a date), or **strike** (it doesn't matter anymore)
3. Physically rewrite migrated tasks into the new month/week

The act of rewriting is intentional friction. If a task isn't worth rewriting, it isn't worth doing. This is the built-in filter that prevents list bloat.

### 4. Reflection

The journal isn't just a task manager — it's a record. Flipping back through past entries reveals patterns: what you keep migrating (maybe it's important, maybe it's guilt), what you keep striking (maybe stop generating that type of task), what you actually completed (more than you think).

## Key Concepts

- **ADHD-native design** — created specifically to manage scattered attention. Short bullets, no elaborate systems, no digital distractions.
- **Intentional friction** — writing by hand forces you to decide if a task is worth the effort of re-recording. Digital systems make copying free, which means nothing gets filtered.
- **Migration as filtering** — the most important concept. The monthly/weekly rewrite is not busywork — it's the decision point where you separate signal from noise.
- **Analog as a feature** — no notifications, no infinite scroll, no "just check one thing" that becomes 30 minutes of distraction. The notebook is a dead-end by design.
- **Rapid logging = low barrier to capture** — the notation is fast enough to not interrupt flow. Capture first, organize later.

## How I Apply It

- **Migration principle applies digitally too.** During the weekly review, anything on the list for 2+ weeks without progress faces the migration question: do it this week, delegate it, or delete it. If you keep migrating it, either it's important (schedule it) or it's guilt (kill it).
- **Rapid logging for daily check-ins.** Milou's evening check-in at 9:30 PM is a rapid log — bullets for what happened, what shipped, what's stuck. Brief, not elaborate.
- **Reflection reveals patterns.** Reviewing a month of rapid logs shows where energy actually went vs. where it was planned to go. This feeds the [Time Allocation](../systems/time-allocation.md) weekly review.
- **The 9:30 PM wind-down is the natural journaling slot.** The schedule already protects this time. BuJo-style reflection fits perfectly here.
- **Collections = brain repo files.** A BuJo collection for "marketing ideas" is the analog equivalent of a brain knowledge file. The brain repo is the digital collection system.
