# Manifesto

> The north star. Everything else derives from this.
> Last updated: 2026-03-31

---

## The Fisherman Parable

A businessman visits a small coastal village and sees a fisherman lounging by his boat. "Why aren't you out fishing?" The fisherman says he already caught enough for the day. The businessman launches into a plan — fish more, buy a fleet, build an empire — "and then you can finally relax and enjoy life." The fisherman says, "What do you think I'm doing right now?"

That's the whole philosophy. I'm not building toward some future state where I finally get to enjoy my life. I'm already there. The goal is to protect that, not sacrifice it for scale.

## What Enough Looks Like

- Family comes first. Always. (Marcie > Kids > Work > Calling > Side projects)
- One-person operation. Home office. No employees, no investors, no board.
- 2.5 hours of maker time per day — that's the real constraint, and it's enough.
- Side income that supplements, not replaces. The day job is not the enemy.
- Seven hours of sleep, time with Marcie, time with the kids — these are non-negotiable.

Enough is not a ceiling. It's a design choice. I'm not limited — I'm focused.

## Build What You Want

No market research. No validation theater. No trend-chasing.

If I want a thing and it doesn't exist the way I want it, I build it. Screen time app for my kids? Build it. Choir accompanist tool? Build it. Shopify bot? Build it. The projects look scattered from the outside — iOS apps, macOS utilities, web tools, interactive essays — but the thread is simple: I wanted each one of these.

Building feels like play. That's not a metaphor. I'm already building my own versions of apps I care about, in my maker time, because I enjoy it. If other people want what I built, great. If not, I still have a tool I use.

This is the Ugmonk lesson: Jeff Sheldon didn't plan a product line. He made a t-shirt he wanted to wear, then a leather mousepad he wanted to use, then a productivity system he needed. Fourteen years later, it's a brand — but only because he kept scratching his own itch.

## Craft Is the Brand

The work speaks. No logo needed.

I don't need people to know my name. I need the things I make to be good — fast-loading, clean, does one thing well, clearly made by someone who cares. That's the digital equivalent of an Ugmonk leather mousepad: you pick it up and you can feel the quality.

"No drama. No preaching. Just craft."

The unifying thread across all my projects isn't visual — a screen time app looks nothing like an interactive essay about CRDTs. The thread is the craft philosophy: thoughtful, well-made, solves a real problem, no bloat. The brand lives in the site, the writeups, the voice — not in the products themselves.

## Ship, Then See

Don't plan the product line. Ship one thing, see what resonates, follow that thread.

Connect the dots backward, not forward. The game plan is not to build a broad portfolio of digital products. The game plan is to build whatever I'm drawn to next, ship it, and notice what sticks. The portfolio is the natural output of someone who ships and follows curiosity.

Tiny bets over big launches. Momentum over perfection. A weekend build that ships beats a six-month project that doesn't.

## Invest Like a Poker Player

Most hands, you fold. Some hands, you call. Very rarely, you go all-in.

With 2.5 hours a day, over-investing is more dangerous than under-investing. Three months polishing one thing is three months of other bets you never placed. The discipline isn't in the building — it's in the restraint.

The system: [Tiny Bets](../../../knowledge/mental-models/tiny-bets.md) (Daniel Vassallo) + [Minimum Effective Dose](../../../knowledge/principles/minimum-effective-dose.md) (Tim Ferriss). Try lots of small things at minimum cost. Watch for signal. Invest more in what's already working. Give real craft — Ugmonk-level TLC — only to the very few things that have earned it.

Three tiers:
- **Explore** — ship the MED. Weekend build. See if anyone cares.
- **Double Down** — real signal? Now it gets real time.
- **Craft** — sustained demand + personal conviction? Now you go Ugmonk. Custom branding, Midjourney assets, hours on the Twitter bio. Maximum two things at this level, ever.

Everything starts at Explore. Promotion is earned, not planned. This is how eleven properties coexist with a full-time job and five kids — most get the minimum, a few get the love, and that's not cutting corners. That's focus.

See [Investment Tiers](../../../knowledge/systems/investment-tiers.md) for the full system.

## Product Is the Content

I don't need a "content strategy." The build writeup IS the content. The demo IS the product. The process IS the story.

The Weekend Build writeup, the dead-end postmortem, the interactive essay — these aren't separate from the work. They're the sawdust from making. The site is the storefront — not a shop, but the place where every build is presented with care. The writeup is the packaging. The demo is the unboxing.

No content calendar. No content treadmill. Build things, write about building things. That's it.

## Stay Small on Purpose

Small is not a phase. It's the destination.

The fisherman doesn't need a fleet. One-person, lean, low overhead. This is a feature, not a limitation. Ugmonk proved that a one-person operation with high taste can build something people love without becoming a company. I have a full-time job, five kids, and real constraints — and those constraints are what keep the work honest.

Resist the "scale at all costs" pressure. Resist the "you should hire" pressure. Resist the "you're leaving money on the table" pressure. The table is fine. The fish is fresh. The kids are playing nearby.

## Decision Filter

When facing a choice, ask:

- **Fisherman or businessman?** Does this optimize for the life I have, or sacrifice it for a future payoff?
- **Do I want this?** Am I building it because I want it, or because I think others want it?
- **Simpler or more complex?** Does this add overhead, or preserve the lean operation?
- **Maker-time compatible?** Can I do this in 2.5 hours a day, or does it require a lifestyle change?
- **Following a thread or forcing a plan?** Am I drawn to this, or am I executing a roadmap?
- **Explore, Double Down, or Craft?** What tier does this belong to — and am I investing accordingly?

If the answer pushes toward the businessman's life, it's a no.
